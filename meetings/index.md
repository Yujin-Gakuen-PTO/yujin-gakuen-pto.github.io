## What PTO meetings are for

PTO meetings give members a shared place to discuss school-community needs, hear reports, ask for clarification, propose work, coordinate support, and make decisions that should not be made by one person alone.

You can attend with a specific question or idea, or simply listen until the process feels familiar.

## How does an idea become PTO action?

<div class="idea-flow">
  <div><strong>Suggest it</strong><p>Email the secretary before the meeting or contact the PTO if you are unsure where the idea belongs.</p></div>
  <div><strong>Describe it</strong><p>Share the need, who it helps, timing, likely cost, and what support would be useful.</p></div>
  <div><strong>Discuss it</strong><p>Members ask questions, identify practical concerns, suggest changes, and decide what should happen next.</p></div>
  <div><strong>Act on it</strong><p>The idea may become a volunteer task, committee project, future agenda item, or formal motion for a vote.</p></div>
</div>

## A few useful terms

<dl class="glossary-list">
  <div><dt>Agenda</dt><dd>The planned order of reports, questions, discussion, and business for a meeting.</dd></div>
  <div><dt>General Assembly</dt><dd>A meeting of PTO members where organization-wide questions, proposals, and decisions can be discussed.</dd></div>
  <div><dt>Motion</dt><dd>A specific proposal for the PTO to take an official action.</dd></div>
  <div><dt>Second</dt><dd>Another member's indication that the motion is worth discussing. It is not automatically a vote in favor.</dd></div>
  <div><dt>Quorum</dt><dd>The minimum participation required by the bylaws for the PTO to conduct official business.</dd></div>
  <div><dt>Table or postpone</dt><dd>Delay a decision so the group can return to it later.</dd></div>
</dl>
